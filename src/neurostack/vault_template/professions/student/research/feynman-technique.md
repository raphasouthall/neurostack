# Feynman Technique

The Feynman Technique uses forced simplification to expose gaps in understanding — if you cannot explain a concept in plain language, you have not truly learned it.

## The Four Steps

1. **Choose a concept** — write the topic name at the top of a blank page
2. **Explain it as if teaching a beginner** — use simple language, no jargon, concrete examples
3. **Identify gaps** — wherever you get stuck, vague, or resort to technical terms, you've found a gap
4. **Return to source material** — study specifically to fill those gaps, then re-explain

## Why It Works

- Explanation is a form of elaborative encoding — you must connect the new idea to things you already know
- The simplification constraint forces you to build a mental model, not just memorise surface features
- Gaps become visible immediately, unlike passive re-reading where the illusion of fluency hides ignorance

## Connection to Active Recall

The technique is a structured application of [[active-recall]] — you generate the explanation from memory rather than copying from notes. Each explanation attempt is a retrieval event that strengthens the memory trace.

## Using the Feynman Technique in This Vault

- When you create a permanent note, the opening paragraph should pass the Feynman test — a plain-language explanation of the concept
- If you cannot write that paragraph without checking the source, the concept needs more study
- Use [[concept-mapping]] to visualise how your Feynman explanations connect to each other

## When to Use

- After lectures, when you suspect surface-level understanding
- Before exams, on topics you've flagged as weak spots in your study guide
- When a [[prediction-errors-in-learning|prediction error]] reveals a concept you thought you knew but didn't
