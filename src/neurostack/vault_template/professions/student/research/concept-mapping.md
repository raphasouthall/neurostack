# Concept Mapping

Concept mapping is the practice of visually representing relationships between ideas as a network of nodes (concepts) and labelled edges (relationships), making the structure of knowledge explicit and examinable.

## Structure of a Concept Map

- **Nodes** — individual concepts, written as short noun phrases
- **Links** — labelled arrows connecting nodes, describing the relationship (e.g., "causes", "is a type of", "requires")
- **Cross-links** — connections between different branches of the map, revealing non-obvious relationships
- **Hierarchy** — general concepts at the top, specific examples at the bottom (though networks need not be strictly hierarchical)

## Why It Works

- Forces you to articulate how concepts relate, not just list them — this is elaborative encoding
- Cross-links are the highest-value element: they connect ideas across domains, which is where insight lives
- Externalises your mental model so you can inspect it for gaps, contradictions, and missing connections
- Mirrors how memory actually works — as an associative network, not a linear list (see [[complementary-learning-systems]])

## How to Use for Studying

1. After a lecture or reading, list the key concepts without looking at notes ([[active-recall]])
2. Arrange them spatially and draw labelled connections
3. Compare your map to the source material — missing links reveal gaps in understanding
4. Revisit and extend the map as the course progresses — watch the network grow

## Concept Mapping in This Vault

- Wiki-links between notes create a digital concept map — each `[[link]]` is an edge in your knowledge graph
- Run `neurostack graph` to visualise your vault's network structure and identify isolated clusters
- Notes with few incoming links may be poorly integrated — add cross-links to strengthen them
- The [[interleaving]] principle applies: connect notes across courses and topics, not just within them

## Tools

- **Pen and paper** — fastest for initial brainstorming
- **Excalidraw** (Obsidian plugin) — embed visual maps directly in notes
- **Mermaid diagrams** — text-based graphs that render in Obsidian

## Limitations

- Time-intensive for large topics — use selectively for high-value or confusing material
- Can become cluttered without discipline — limit to 15-20 nodes per map
- The map is only as good as the labels on the links — vague connections ("is related to") add little value
