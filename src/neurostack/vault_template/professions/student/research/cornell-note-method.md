# Cornell Note Method

The Cornell method divides a page into three regions — cue column, notes area, and summary — to transform passive lecture capture into an active learning cycle.

## Structure

- **Notes column** (right, ~70% width) — record lecture content during class in your own words, not verbatim
- **Cue column** (left, ~30% width) — after class, write questions or keywords that correspond to the notes opposite them
- **Summary** (bottom) — within 24 hours, write a 2-3 sentence summary of the page in your own words

## Why It Works

- The cue column forces you to identify the core idea behind each section — a form of [[active-recall]] at the encoding stage
- Writing the summary after class is a retrieval event that strengthens [[memory-consolidation]]
- The cues become a built-in self-test: cover the notes column and try to answer each cue from memory

## How to Use in This Vault

- The lecture note template mirrors Cornell structure: Key Concepts (cues), Notes (main content), Summary (bottom)
- After each lecture, review your note and convert key ideas into permanent notes with [[wiki-links]]
- Schedule reviews using [[spaced-repetition]] intervals: 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 21 days

## Limitations

- Not ideal for highly visual or mathematical content — supplement with diagrams or worked examples
- The method optimises for factual recall; for deep understanding, combine with [[feynman-technique]]
- Requires discipline to complete the cue and summary steps — most of the benefit comes from those, not the initial notes
