# Show, Don't Tell

"Show, don't tell" is the most repeated and most misunderstood writing advice. It does not mean never tell. It means: when the reader needs to *feel* something, render it through concrete detail and action rather than summary.

## When to Show

- Emotional beats — the reader must experience the feeling, not be informed of it
- Character-defining moments — actions reveal character more reliably than narration
- Key plot turns — the reader needs to be present, not told what happened

## When Telling is Better

- Transitions — "Three weeks later" is better than dramatising every day
- Low-stakes information — backstory, logistics, context that would bore if dramatised
- Pacing control — a paragraph of summary can compress time between high-tension scenes

## Techniques for Showing

### Concrete Sensory Detail

**Telling**: She was nervous.
**Showing**: She picked at the label on her water bottle until it came away in wet strips.

### Subtext in Dialogue

**Telling**: He was angry about the promotion.
**Showing**: "No, that's great news. Really. I'm happy for you." He set his fork down and pushed his plate away.

### Action as Character

**Telling**: She was generous.
**Showing**: She left the biggest portion in the pan and served herself last.

### Environment as Emotion

**Telling**: The town was dying.
**Showing**: Half the storefronts on Main Street had brown paper taped inside the windows. A wind chime hung from a nail in a doorframe with no door.

## The Real Rule

The audience should *arrive at the conclusion themselves*. Showing works because it respects the reader's intelligence. Telling works when the conclusion is not the point and the reader needs to move forward.
