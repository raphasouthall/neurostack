# Voice and Tone

Voice is the writer's fingerprint — the consistent quality that makes a sentence recognisably *yours* regardless of subject. Tone is the emotional register of a specific piece. Voice is permanent; tone shifts by project, scene, or sentence.

## Elements of Voice

- **Diction**: Word choice — Latinate vs Anglo-Saxon, abstract vs concrete, formal vs colloquial
- **Syntax**: Sentence structure — short and declarative, long and subordinate, fragmented
- **Rhythm**: The cadence when read aloud — staccato, flowing, syncopated
- **Perspective**: How close the narrator sits to the character's mind
- **Obsessions**: The images, metaphors, and concerns a writer returns to involuntarily

## Developing Voice

Voice is not invented; it is discovered through volume. Strategies:

1. **Write a lot** — voice emerges from the accumulated weight of decisions. There is no shortcut
2. **Copy by hand** — transcribe passages from writers whose voice you admire. Your hand teaches your ear
3. **Read your work aloud** — the mouth catches what the eye forgives. If you wouldn't say it, don't write it
4. **Identify your defaults** — notice your habitual sentence structures, then deliberately vary them
5. **Write the same scene in three different voices** — constraint reveals preference

## Tone Control

Tone is a dial, not a switch. It is controlled through:

- **Sentence length**: Short sentences raise tension. Longer, more complex sentences create a contemplative or lyrical register that slows the reader down.
- **Specificity**: Precise detail creates intimacy; abstraction creates distance
- **Pacing**: Fast scenes use short paragraphs and white space. Slow scenes use density
- **Diction register**: Formal/informal, technical/plain — must match POV character's mind

## Common Pitfalls

- **Inconsistent register**: Shifting between formal and casual within a single POV without motivation
- **Overwriting**: Trying to make every sentence beautiful. Some sentences exist to carry the reader to the next beautiful one
- **Voice vs affectation**: Voice is natural. If it feels performed, it is affectation. Affectation exhausts the reader
- **Losing voice in revision**: Over-editing can sand away the texture that made the prose alive. Compare revised passages to the raw draft
