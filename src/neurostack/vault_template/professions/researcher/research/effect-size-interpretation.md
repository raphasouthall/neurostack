# Effect Size Interpretation

Effect sizes quantify the magnitude of a phenomenon independent of sample size, making them essential for comparing results across studies and powering meta-analyses.

## Common Measures

| Measure | Type | Small | Medium | Large |
|---------|------|-------|--------|-------|
| Cohen's d | Mean difference | 0.2 | 0.5 | 0.8 |
| Pearson's r | Correlation | 0.1 | 0.3 | 0.5 |
| Odds ratio | Binary outcome | 1.5 | 2.5 | 4.3 |
| η² | Variance explained | 0.01 | 0.06 | 0.14 |

## Why Effect Sizes Over P-Values

- P-values conflate effect magnitude with sample size — a trivial effect becomes "significant" with enough participants
- Effect sizes enable **cumulative science** — they can be aggregated across studies via meta-analysis
- Confidence intervals around effect sizes communicate both magnitude and precision

## Practical Significance

Cohen's benchmarks are arbitrary starting points. Always interpret effect sizes in domain context:
- A d=0.2 intervention that costs nothing and scales to millions of people may be transformative
- A d=0.8 intervention requiring years of individual therapy may be impractical
