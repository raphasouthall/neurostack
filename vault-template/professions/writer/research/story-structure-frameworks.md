# Story Structure Frameworks

Structure is not formula. It is the underlying rhythm that readers subconsciously expect — a framework for creating and releasing tension. The right structure depends on genre, tone, and what the story is trying to do.

## Three-Act Structure

The default in Western storytelling. Works because it maps to setup-confrontation-resolution.

- **Act 1 (25%)**: Establish normal world, introduce protagonist, inciting incident forces a choice
- **Act 2 (50%)**: Rising stakes, midpoint reversal, escalating obstacles, crisis
- **Act 3 (25%)**: Climax resolves the central dramatic question, new equilibrium

**Best for**: Commercial fiction, screenplays, short stories with clear arcs.

## Save the Cat (Snyder)

A beat sheet that prescribes specific turning points at specific page percentages. Useful as training wheels, dangerous as a cage.

- Opening Image / Theme Stated / Setup / Catalyst / Debate / Break into Two / B Story / Fun and Games / Midpoint / Bad Guys Close In / All Is Lost / Dark Night of the Soul / Break into Three / Finale / Final Image

**Best for**: Genre fiction, screenwriting, writers who freeze without a roadmap.

## Kishotenketsu (Four-Act)

Japanese/Chinese/Korean structure built on contrast rather than conflict.

1. **Ki** — Introduction
2. **Sho** — Development
3. **Ten** — Twist (an unexpected element, not necessarily a conflict)
4. **Ketsu** — Reconciliation (the reader synthesises the twist with what came before)

**Best for**: Literary fiction, slice-of-life, stories where theme matters more than plot.

## The Story Circle (Harmon)

Dan Harmon's simplified Hero's Journey: You-Need-Go-Search-Find-Take-Return-Change. Eight beats around a circle, crossing the threshold at the midpoint.

**Best for**: Episodic storytelling, character-driven narratives, series structure.

## Choosing a Framework

- Start with the feeling you want the reader to have at the end
- If the story is about transformation through conflict — three-act or Save the Cat
- If the story is about revelation or juxtaposition — Kishotenketsu
- If you are structuring a series or episodic work — Story Circle
- You can hybridise: use Kishotenketsu's twist as the midpoint in a three-act structure
