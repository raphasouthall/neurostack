# Pomodoro Technique

The Pomodoro Technique structures study into 25-minute focused intervals separated by short breaks, using time pressure and rest to sustain attention and prevent burnout.

## Protocol

1. Choose a single task — one assignment, one set of problems, one chapter
2. Set a timer for 25 minutes (one "pomodoro")
3. Work with full focus — no phone, no tab-switching, no interruptions
4. When the timer rings, take a 5-minute break — stand, stretch, look away from screens
5. After four pomodoros, take a 15-30 minute break
6. Track completed pomodoros to build awareness of how long tasks actually take

## Why It Works

- **Parkinson's Law** — work expands to fill the time available; a 25-minute constraint creates urgency
- **Attention is finite** — sustained focus degrades after ~20-30 minutes; scheduled breaks prevent diminishing returns
- **Diffuse mode** — breaks allow the brain to enter diffuse processing, which supports [[memory-consolidation]] and creative problem-solving
- **Progress visibility** — counting pomodoros gives tangible evidence of effort, reducing procrastination anxiety

## Adaptations for Deep Work

- For complex tasks (proofs, essays, coding), extend to 50-minute blocks with 10-minute breaks
- Pair with [[retrieval-practice]]: spend the first pomodoro testing yourself, the second reviewing gaps
- Use breaks for spaced review: revisit flashcards from [[spaced-repetition]] during the 5-minute window

## Limitations

- Rigid 25-minute blocks can interrupt flow states — if you're deep in a proof, finish the thought before breaking
- Not suited for collaborative work or lab sessions with fixed schedules
- The technique manages time, not comprehension — combine with [[feynman-technique]] to ensure depth
